Learn the basics of GDPR

Why it matters: Ignoring GDPR regulation can result to fines of up to million euros, or up to 4% of the organisation’s turnover for the previous financial year, whichever is greater. A lot of privacy requirements are based around GDPR.

The background: The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) is a privacy and security law in with the purpose of protecting data and privacy within the EU and the EEA.

  • The GDPR’s main aim is to maximise an individual’s control over their personal and sensible information, while also simplifying the regulatory environment for international business.

In practice: In the upcoming sections, you will get a quick rundown of some of the basic requirements of GDPR.

Is your privacy policy sufficient?

Why it matters: A clear privacy policy that is easy to find is a requirement under the GDPR.

In practice:

  • There are numerous privacy policy templates available online, but many of them fail to meet the GDPR requirements of clear and plain language.
  • The typical placement of a link to a privacy policy is in the footer at the bottom of the page. By following this convention, your users can find it easily.

Ask consent for cookies

Why it matters:If your website does not ask its user for consent on cookies, it’s most likely not GDPR compliant. By having clear cookie options, you are telling the user that you care about their data privacy.

The background: Cookies are small text files that websites use to track user behaviour and preferences. For example, to prevent the user having to log in on every page, the computer stores a cookie that the user is logged in for easy navigation. Cookies can also be from third parties, like social media plugins or Google’s services.

  • Under GDPR, all third-party and most first-party cookies require consent.
  • It is common to hide the cookie options under settings and nudge the user to allow all cookies with colors and positioning of buttons. These are dark patterns and should be avoided.

In practice: When user enters the website, there should be a cookie prompt to ask if the user consents for the use of statistical or advertising cookies. It is a good practice to present cookie options equally, in the same menu and same color.

Be careful with forms

Why it matters: Forms are vulnerable to data breaches, cyber-attacks, and unauthorized access by third-party. Some forms also sell data to data brokers.

In practice: Forms should only ask for information that is strictly necessary and avoid sensitive data such as BSNs. Only create a form with a reliable provider.

Reconsider using Google Analytics

Why it matters: Multiple privacy concerns have been raised regarding performance metrics like Google Analytics.

The background: While tools like Google Analytics can help to identify trends, and measure the effectiveness of marketing strategies, they can pose threats to privacy. These analysis services collect sensible information such as geolocation and device information.

  • Third-parties can be intrusive when it comes to personally identifiable information. The data can end up being used by advertisers, which allows them to use targeted advertising without the user’s knowledge or consent.

In practice: Avoid using Google Analytics. If you still choose to use it, adjust the settings to be as privacy aware as possible.

Alternative tools

Website analytics: Simple Analytics

Pros: Doesn’t collect even anonymised data from your website visitors. Fast loading time. Based in the Netherlands. Easy installation guide.

Cons: Paid. Very simplified.

Font-hosting: Host font files locally

Pros: Privacy-friendly. No reliance on third parties.

Cons: Requires some knowledge to set up. Requires testing to see if fonts are visible with all browsers.

Additional resources: OpenKat - vulnerability analysis tool for complex systems

Pros: Open source. Constantly developing new features. Privacy-first approach.

Cons: Requires technical knowledge to set up.

About us


The Coffeebreak Crash Course on Website Privacy is the work of three students from Computational Social Science of University of Amsterdam, with the mission to promote a privacy-aware world. We hope to provide easy to understand and practical information on privacy issues related to websites, and we wish this serves as an inspiration to carry more private practices everywhere.

The project started with a challenge from Sigra, a partnership of healthcare organisations in North Holland, and OpenKat, a vulnerability analysis tool for networks developed under the Dutch Ministry of Health. The goal was to develop a digital intervention to increase privacy practices within the healthcare industry in the region.
